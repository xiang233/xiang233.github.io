a few words on procrastination

Posted on 2020-10-15 Edited on 2020-10-24

Is there really any distinction between the urgent and not urgent, the important and not important? Most would probably believe so. The belief has such a presence that, even with an eye closed, you could still sense if in the air. Or at least I could.

[^the famous Eisenhower Matrix]: I constantly land and dwell on the fourth quadrant …Sorry.

I could sense it when that, if you are having a single trait or bearing a slight resemblance to the widely recognised image of a model student, you are expected to strive and behave like one, as if it is some crowning accolade. You are asked to focus on exams, on family, on sports, to stand upright and smile politely like Annie Hathaway and seek all the highest achievements like Ivanka Trump. Meticulously maintaining this facade, relishing the moment when everyone’s envious gazes attempt to pull it to crumble and break apart, while it is remaining perfectly still.

When I was a kid, I often asked my mom whether I could become a hairdresser, a philosopher, an economist, anything. She always replied, “of course you can,” but strangely followed by, “I believe you would be successful in any field you choose to engage in.” Yet what if I fail to, or don’t desperately want to, succeed? What if I would devote my whole life to a theory that gains no worthwhile result, or what if I’d rather wander aimlessly among a multitude of knowledge, where the possibility of discovery comes solely by chance?

In my world there thus exists such distinctions. And it sounds reasonable that Eisenhower would, apart from categorising them, also categorise me as an incorrigible procrastinator. As I often resort to art, music and trivial writings, where the need to prioritise is absent.

Sometimes when deeply engrossed, I feel the urge to leave everything behind, plunge fully into the moment and not retract, or to shift completely to a new approach or perspective towards a situation. So, I thought, what about re-defining these categories and prioritising scheme, by myself, for my own life? But changes always come at a cost - as Sabina Spielrein said, changes are inevitably associated with destruction of the former state. When the results of a newly established habit are no longer predictable, I hesitate to take the risk. Yes…I procrastinate during ‘urgent and important’ work, and I procrastinate even during procrastination.

While, a life span is long and not every day is an HSC or SAT preparation day. There is always time to experiment with novelty, so why not start changing now?
